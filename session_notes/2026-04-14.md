## Task: Across-Study Correlation vs Traditional Surrogate Methods

### Summary

Clarified how our correlation functional cor(Δ_S(Q), Δ_Y(Q)) relates to traditional surrogate measures (PTE, mediation) and constructed explicit scenarios where they diverge.

### Corrected Understanding

**Initial confusion:** Thought our correlation was ρ(S₀,S₁; Y₀,Y₁) within a single study

**Actual setup:**
- Our correlation: cor(E[Y₁ - Y₀], E[S₁ - S₀]) **across studies**
- Studies differ by X distribution: Q determines P(X)
- Δ_S(Q) = E_Q[Δ_S(X)], Δ_Y(Q) = E_Q[Δ_Y(X)]
- These are linear functionals (reweightings) of the heterogeneous effects

### Two Divergence Scenarios Constructed ✓

#### Scenario 1: High ρ, Low PTE
**Mechanism:** Δ_S(X) and Δ_Y(X) both vary with X, but S doesn't cause Y

**Results:**
- Across-study ρ = 1.0 (perfect correlation)
- Within-study PTE = 0.072 (very low mediation)

**Interpretation:**
- Knowing Δ_S(Q) perfectly predicts Δ_Y(Q) across studies
- But S doesn't mediate Y within a study
- X is a common driver of both effect heterogeneity patterns

#### Scenario 2: Undefined ρ, High PTE
**Mechanism:** Δ_S(X) constant (no heterogeneity), Δ_Y(X) varies, but S strongly causes Y

**Results:**
- Across-study ρ = NA (no variation in Δ_S to correlate)
- Within-study PTE = 0.8 (strong mediation)

**Interpretation:**
- S strongly mediates Y within a study
- But Δ_S provides no predictive information across studies (constant)
- Effect modification exists only in Δ_Y(X), not Δ_S(X)

### Mathematical Formulation

**Core relationships:**
- Δ_S(Q) = E_Q[Δ_S(X)] and Δ_Y(Q) = E_Q[Δ_Y(X)] (linear functionals)
- Across-study correlation: depends on Cov(Δ_S(X), Δ_Y(X)) across X values
- PTE: depends on how much of E[Y | A, X] is explained by E[Y | A, S, X]
- These capture different properties of the DGP

**When they diverge:**
1. Δ_S(X) and Δ_Y(X) co-vary through common driver X, but S doesn't cause Y → High ρ, Low PTE
2. S causes Y strongly, but Δ_S(X) constant (no X-interaction) → Undefined/Low ρ, High PTE

### Summary

### Major Changes Completed

#### 1. Setting Section (lines 83-105) ✅

**Before:** "Defining the random probability measure $\cF$"
- Innovation mechanism: $\cF_\lambda(\PP_0) = \{\Q = (1-\lambda)\PP_0 + \lambda\tilde{\PP}\}$
- Focus on mixture representation
- Parameter $\lambda$ controls mixture weight

**After:** "Local geometries for future studies"
- Direct distance metric: $U(\PP_0, \lambda; d) = \{\Q : d(\Q, \PP_0) \leq \lambda\}$
- Listed common metrics: TV, KL, chi-squared, L2
- Focus on general distance-based neighborhoods
- Uniform measure $\mu$ on $U(\PP_0, \lambda; d)$

**Impact:** Framework now clearly general across any distance metric, not tied to TV-specific mixture representation.

#### 2. Estimation Section (lines 176-343) ✅

**Computational approach replaced:**
- **Before:** Dirichlet sampling from innovation distribution
- **After:** Hit-and-run MCMC for uniform sampling from convex geometries

**Algorithm added:**
1. Draw random direction in simplex
2. Compute line segment within TV ball
3. Sample uniformly along line
4. Project to simplex
5. Burn-in + convergence diagnostics

**Treatment effect estimation - Critical addition:**

**RCTs (simple case):**
```
Δ̂_S(Q_m) = Σ w_i A_i S_i / Σ w_i A_i - Σ w_i (1-A_i) S_i / Σ w_i (1-A_i)
```
where $w_i = q_m(O_i)/p_0(O_i)$ is importance weight.

**Observational studies (new):**
```
Δ̂_S(Q_m) = Σ w_i [A_i(S_i - μ̂_1^S(X_i))/ê(X_i) - (1-A_i)(S_i - μ̂_0^S(X_i))/(1-ê(X_i))
             + μ̂_1^S(X_i) - μ̂_0^S(X_i)]
```
**Key:** Cross-fitting ensures nuisance parameters estimated on independent data, enabling asymptotic normality under weak conditions (Chernozhukov et al., 2018).

**Why this matters:** Original version only had weighted means (appropriate for RCTs). Real-world surrogate evaluation often uses observational data. AIPW with cross-fitting is doubly robust and removes nuisance parameter estimation bias from first-order asymptotics.

**Variance decomposition updated:**
- **Before:** 3 sources (P₀ estimation, MC error, treatment effect estimation)
- **After:** 2 sources (P₀ estimation including nuisance parameters, MC error from MCMC)
- Unified treatment: cross-fitting makes nuisance estimation $O_p(n^{-1/2})$
- MC error negligible when $M = o(n)$, so $M = O(n^{1/2})$ suffices

#### 3. Asymptotic Theory (lines 293-330) ✅

**New proposition structure:**

**Estimand:** $\Theta(\PP_0) = \E_{\text{Uniform}}[\phi(\Q) \mid \Q \in U(\PP_0, \lambda; d)]$

**Two-stage proof sketch:**

**Stage 1: Treatment effects**
- RCTs: Weighted means with influence function $\psi_{\Delta_S}(O; \Q) = w(\Q, O)[A \cdot S - (1-A) \cdot S - \Delta_S(\Q)]$
- Observational: AIPW with cross-fitting removes nuisance bias

**Stage 2: Functional aggregation**
- Apply functional delta method to $\phi$ (e.g., correlation)
- Hadamard differentiability (continuous functionals on bivariate distributions)
- Average over $M = o(n)$ MCMC draws
- Ergodicity of hit-and-run yields overall influence function

**Result:** $\sqrt{n}(\hat{\phi}_n(\lambda) - \Theta(\PP_0)) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, \sigma^2(\lambda))$

where $\sigma^2(\lambda) = \E[\psi_\Theta(O; \PP_0)^2]$

**Note:** Complete derivation deferred to future work, but key insight is cross-fitting enables standard functional delta method without nuisance parameter complications.
